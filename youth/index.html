As Afghanistan rebuilds, it needs its young people more than ever. Short life expectancy, conflict-induced migration, and death in conflict has left Afghanistan with a critical lack of experience and expertise. But while young Afghans are needed to fill many positions, the same conditions have made their lives extremely difficult, taking a heavy toll on the education system, the economy, and life opportunities.

As Afghanistan's political system evolves, young people could also bring new ideas -- and aspirations to the table. However traditionally Afghan youth have not always had opportunities to add their voices to debate.

For simple demographic reasons, many media sector workers and citizen journalists are young people. But Internews has also created specific initiatived for young Afghans to tell their stories and be part of the national civil dialogue about the future of their country. These include dedicated youth programs on the Salam Watandar national radio service and the Afghan Youth Voices Festival. The festival is a 6-month event, involving training, mentoring, equipment access and eventually a focus week of youth media and arts activity. The website for the 2011 festival is here and for the 2010 festival is here.

Youth engagement is also part of the AMDEP baseline studies conducted by Altai.
